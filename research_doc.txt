Abstract
Underwater ship-radiated noise recorded by hydrophone sensors is a composite acoustic signal comprising mechanical machinery vibrations, propeller cavitation noise, hydrodynamic flow noise, hull structural resonances, and ambient ocean noise. Accurately decomposing this mixture into individual source contributions is of critical importance for naval acoustic intelligence, submarine detection, vessel classification, and fleet signature management. This document presents a research-grade, end-to-end Generative AI framework for (i) decomposing measured Radiated Noise Level (RNL) signals into constituent Ship-Borne Noise (SBN) source components; (ii) estimating per-source contribution weights via physics-informed mixture modelling; (iii) characterising and modelling non-Gaussian residual noise using advanced generative architectures; and (iv) synthesising realistic acoustic training data to overcome data scarcity constraints inherent in naval environments. The proposed pipeline integrates classical signal processing (FFT, Welch PSD, STFT, cepstral analysis, NMF) with state-of-the-art Generative AI methods including Conditional Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM), Wasserstein GANs with Gradient Penalty (WGAN-GP), Temporal GANs (TimeGAN), Variational Autoencoders (VAE), and Transformer-based attention mechanisms. The framework is validated on the DeepShip dataset comprising 47+ hours of real-world underwater recordings across four vessel classes. The ultimate operational output is a vessel classification system of demonstrably superior accuracy, providing the Indian Navy with a robust, deployable acoustic intelligence capability.

Keywords: Underwater acoustics, ship-radiated noise, source decomposition, NMF, conditional diffusion model, WGAN-GP, TimeGAN, VAE, RNL-SBN mapping, naval acoustic intelligence, DeepShip, hydrophone signal processing

1.  Introduction
Modern naval operations demand precise acoustic intelligence on surface and sub-surface threats. A hydrophone deployed below a naval vessel or on the seabed records a single composite time-domain signal that encodes acoustic energy emitted by every rotating, vibrating, and cavitating component aboard the target ship. Traditional signal processing pipelines — based on Fast Fourier Transforms, simple spectral subtraction, or Independent Component Analysis — fall short of reliably isolating individual source contributions, especially under adverse sea conditions, high ambient noise, and non-stationary machinery states.

The advent of Generative Artificial Intelligence presents a transformative opportunity. Unlike discriminative models that merely label or classify, generative models learn the underlying probability distributions of acoustic signals. This capability enables the framework to perform source separation in situations where no clean reference signal exists, to fill data gaps in underrepresented vessel classes, and to synthesise realistic noise environments for testing and classifier training without endangering naval assets.

This document defines the complete research architecture — mathematical foundations, signal processing pipeline, generative AI module design, evaluation methodology, and implementation roadmap — for an operational acoustic intelligence system.

1.1  Motivation and Operational Context
A ship operating at sea generates noise from multiple independent sources simultaneously. The hydrophone records only the superposition:
x(t) = Engine(t) + Generator(t) + Pump(t) + Propeller(t) + Cavitation(t) + HullVibration(t) + AmbientNoise(t)
The key operational questions are:
Which machinery components are present and active?
What is the relative acoustic contribution (weight) of each component?
What residual non-Gaussian noise remains after accounting for identified components?
Can this residual be modelled and synthesised to augment classifier training data?
Can a downstream classifier reliably identify vessel class and type from the decomposed signatures?

1.2  Scope and Limitations
The present framework addresses single-hydrophone (single-channel) recordings. Multi-channel beamforming extensions are noted where applicable but are reserved for future work. The physical model adopted is a linear additive mixture with frequency-dependent hull transfer function — an approximation that is well-supported by literature for moderate sea states and does not account for strong multipath arrivals. Extensions to handle severe multipath, Doppler shifts from moving targets, and classified sonar architectures are outside the immediate scope of this public research document.

2.2  Deficiencies of the Simplified Model
A naive simplification RNL - SBN = w^T(M) fails on seven physical grounds:
RNL already contains SBN: subtracting SBN from RNL does not isolate propagation effects.
Propagation loss is ignored: the hydrophone is at distance r from the source; geometric and absorption losses are frequency-dependent.
Cavitation is non-linear: bubble collapse produces broadband impulsive noise not captured by a linear mixture.
Hull transfer function H(f) is vessel-specific, unknown without calibration, and varies with loading.
Environmental effects E(f) include multipath arrivals, creating spectral interference patterns.
Sea state modulates ambient noise A(f) dynamically (Beaufort scale dependency).
Non-stationarity: engine RPM, vessel speed, and loading change over a recording, making w_i time-varying.

2.3  Research Objectives
Given the above, the following formal objectives are defined:
Source Decomposition (SD): Recover estimated component signals M_i(t) from observed mixture x(t) without ground-truth individual source recordings (blind/semi-blind separation).
Contribution Weight Estimation (CWE): Estimate scalar weights w_i representing each source's fractional contribution to total measured RNL.
RNL-SBN Mapping (RSM): Estimate the hull transfer function H(f) and propagation model to map from measured RNL back to constituent SBN source levels.
Residual Noise Characterisation (RNC): Characterise the distribution of the residual signal after subtracting the weighted source model.
Generative Noise Modelling (GNM): Train generative models (WGAN-GP, Diffusion, TimeGAN) to synthesise realistic residual noise samples.
Synthetic Data Augmentation (SDA): Produce augmented training corpora for the downstream vessel classifier.
Vessel Classification (VC): Train and evaluate classification models on the augmented dataset to achieve state-of-the-art accuracy on the DeepShip benchmark.

3.  Literature Review
3.1  Classical Signal Processing for Underwater Acoustics
Traditional approaches to underwater acoustic analysis rely on deterministic signal processing tools. The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) converts the time-domain hydrophone signal to the frequency domain, exposing dominant spectral peaks corresponding to rotating machinery. The Welch Power Spectral Density (PSD) estimator improves on single-window FFT by averaging periodograms computed from overlapping segments, significantly reducing spectral variance — particularly important for broadband ship noise where signal-to-noise ratios can be as low as -5 dB.

Cepstral analysis — computing the IFFT of the log power spectrum — has been widely employed for detecting harmonic families produced by rotating machinery, by transforming convolved spectral patterns into additive quefrency-domain features. The Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) provides the time-frequency representation necessary to track non-stationary source behaviour as vessel speed and engine load change during a recording.

3.2  Blind Source Separation
Independent Component Analysis (ICA) and its variants were among the first machine learning tools applied to underwater acoustic separation. ICA exploits statistical independence between source signals to recover them from the mixture. Key work by Mansour et al. (2006) applied ICA to passive acoustic tomography, while Zhang et al. (2011) used BSS to cancel tug interference from hydrophone recordings. A fundamental limitation of ICA is that the number of sensors must equal or exceed the number of sources — a constraint violated in most single-hydrophone deployments.

Non-negative Matrix Factorisation (NMF) provides a more flexible decomposition framework, factorising the spectrogram matrix V ≈ WH into non-negative basis spectra W and time-varying activation coefficients H. This avoids the sensor count constraint and produces interpretable spectral templates. NMF has been demonstrated to recover machinery-specific spectral profiles from composite ship noise, though it requires careful initialisation and is sensitive to the number of components chosen.

3.3  Deep Learning for Acoustic Separation
The Conv-TasNet architecture (Luo & Mesgarani, 2019) established a new benchmark for single-channel audio source separation by operating directly in the time domain. Using 1D dilated convolutions to construct separation masks, Conv-TasNet surpassed ideal time-frequency masking methods. He et al. (2024) extended Conv-TasNet with parallel dilated convolution and group convolution specifically for ship-radiated noise, achieving SNR of 16.86 dB and SI-SNRi of 2.53 dB on the DeepShip dataset with a model size of only 3.67M parameters — outperforming both Res-UNet (103M) and UNet (33M).

DCUNet combines deep complex networks with UNet architectures to estimate complex ratio masks in the time-frequency domain, preserving phase information. Res-UNet employs complex ideal ratio masks to address sensitivity to temporal displacement. These architectures, while originally developed for speech separation, have shown adaptability to underwater acoustic separation when retrained on domain-specific data.

3.4  Generative AI Approaches
The Generative Adversarial Network (GAN) framework, introduced by Goodfellow et al. (2014), enables learning of complex implicit distributions without a tractable likelihood. For underwater acoustics, GANs have been applied to noise distribution learning (Xiamen sea GAN study documented in the reference material), where a CNN-based discriminator combined with a dense generator achieved the lowest MSE, KL, and JS divergence scores compared to GMM and alpha-stable baselines.

Wasserstein GANs with Gradient Penalty (WGAN-GP) address mode collapse and training instability by replacing the Jensen-Shannon divergence loss with the Wasserstein-1 distance, enforced via gradient penalty rather than weight clipping. This is particularly relevant for heavy-tailed, impulsive underwater noise distributions that frustrate standard GAN training.

Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPM) have recently surpassed GANs in sample quality and distribution coverage across many domains. The forward diffusion process gradually corrupts data with Gaussian noise; the learned reverse process denoises via a neural score function. Conditional extensions (classifier-free guidance) allow source-label-conditioned generation, making them ideal for controlled acoustic source synthesis.

TimeGAN (Yoon et al., 2019) combines supervised and adversarial objectives with an embedding/recovery architecture to capture temporal dynamics of sequential data, making it well-suited for time-series acoustic generation where temporal correlations are physically meaningful.

3.5  DeepShip Dataset
The DeepShip dataset, assembled by Northwestern Polytechnical University, provides 47 hours and 4 minutes of real-world underwater recordings from 265 different ships across four vessel classes (Cargo, Passengership, Tanker, Tug), captured at the Strait of Georgia delta node between May 2016 and October 2018. It captures diverse seasonal conditions, ambient marine mammal noise, and varying sea states, making it the most comprehensive publicly available benchmark for underwater vessel acoustic analysis. All generative and classification results in this framework are validated against DeepShip.

4.  Mathematical Framework
4.1  Signal Model and Mixture Formulation
Let x(t) ∈ R be the single-channel hydrophone recording sampled at frequency F_s. The discrete-time model is:
x[n] = Σ(i=1 to N) w_i · m_i[n]  +  ε[n],    n = 0, 1, ..., L-1
where m_i[n] are latent source signals, w_i ∈ R^+ are non-negative contribution weights, and ε[n] is the residual noise process. In the frequency domain under the short-time Fourier transform:
X(k, τ) = Σ(i=1 to N) w_i · M_i(k, τ)  +  E(k, τ)
where k indexes frequency bins and τ indexes time frames.

4.2  Welch PSD Decomposition
Given K overlapping segments of length L_seg with hop size H, the Welch PSD estimate is:
S_xx(k) = (1/K) Σ(j=0 to K-1) |X_j(k)|²  /  ( F_s · Σ w²[n] )
The NMF decomposition on the PSD matrix S ∈ R^(F×T) is:
S ≈ W · H,    W ∈ R^(F×R)_≥0,    H ∈ R^(R×T)_≥0
where R is the number of latent components. The update rules (multiplicative Lee-Seung) are:
W ← W ⊙ [(S / WH) · H^T] / [1_F · H^T]
H ← H ⊙ [W^T · (S / WH)] / [W^T · 1_F]

4.3  Source Contribution Estimation
Given recovered component spectral templates W[:,r], the contribution weight for source i at frame τ is estimated by solving a non-negative least squares (NNLS) problem:
w* = argmin_w  ||S[:,τ] - W·w||²_2    subject to  w ≥ 0
The time-averaged weights then give scalar RNL contributions. The hull transfer function H(f) is estimated in the frequency domain via:
H(f) = S_yx(f) / S_xx(f)
where S_yx is the cross-PSD between measured RNL and estimated SBN component sum.

4.4  Residual Noise Model
After subtracting the weighted source model, the residual is:
ε[n] = x[n]  -  Σ(i=1 to N) w_i* · m_i[n]
The residual is characterised by its probability density function p_ε. Statistical tests (Kolmogorov-Smirnov, Shapiro-Wilk) determine whether p_ε departs significantly from Gaussianity. Key diagnostics are excess kurtosis κ_4 and skewness γ_1:
κ_4 = E[(ε - μ)^4] / σ^4  -  3,    γ_1 = E[(ε - μ)^3] / σ^3
For κ_4 >> 0 (leptokurtic), an alpha-stable distribution S(α, β, γ, δ) with characteristic exponent α ∈ (0,2] is fitted as an intermediate baseline before WGAN-GP modelling.

4.5  Diffusion Model Score Function
The conditional denoising diffusion model parameterises the score function of the source signal posterior. Forward process:
q(x_t | x_{t-1}) = N(x_t ; √(1-β_t) · x_{t-1},  β_t · I)
Reverse learned denoising process:
p_θ(x_{t-1} | x_t, c) = N(x_{t-1} ; μ_θ(x_t, t, c),  Σ_θ(x_t, t, c))
where c is the conditioning vector (source label, vessel class, operational state). The score network ε_θ is trained by minimising:
L_diffusion = E_{t,x_0,ε} [ ||ε  -  ε_θ(√ᾱ_t · x_0 + √(1-ᾱ_t) · ε,  t,  c)||² ]

4.6  WGAN-GP Objective
The Wasserstein-1 distance between real residual distribution p_r and generated distribution p_g is estimated via:
W(p_r, p_g) = max_{||f||_L ≤ 1} E_{x~p_r}[f(x)]  -  E_{z~p_z}[f(G(z))]
With gradient penalty λ_GP to enforce the 1-Lipschitz constraint:
L_D = E_{z~p_z}[D(G(z))]  -  E_{x~p_r}[D(x)]  +  λ_GP · E_{x̂~p_x̂}[ (||∇_{x̂} D(x̂)||_2 - 1)² ]
L_G = -E_{z~p_z}[D(G(z))]

Stage I — Signal Acquisition and Preprocessing (Phases 1-2)
Phase 1: Signal Loading and Inventory
All WAV files across the four vessel class directories (Cargo, Passengership, Tanker, Tug) are enumerated. Per-file metadata extracted: filename, vessel class, native sample rate F_s, duration T (seconds), bit depth, number of samples L, peak amplitude, and RMS energy level. Results are persisted to outputs/features/inventory.csv. Quality flags are applied: files shorter than 3 seconds, with clipped samples (amplitude > 0.999), or with sample rates below 8 kHz are flagged for manual review.

Phase 2: Preprocessing Chain
Each WAV signal undergoes the following sequential operations:
DC Offset Removal: Subtract the sample mean. x[n] ← x[n] - mean(x). Eliminates hydrophone sensor bias voltage.
Amplitude Normalisation: Peak normalisation. x[n] ← x[n] / max(|x[n]|). Ensures consistent dynamic range across recordings from different hydrophone gain settings.
Bandpass Filtering: 4th-order Butterworth IIR filter with passband 10 Hz – 1000 Hz (configurable). Applied as zero-phase forward-backward filter (scipy.signal.sosfiltfilt) to eliminate DC content and ultrasonic frequencies irrelevant to machinery signatures. The lower cutoff of 10 Hz preserves infrasonic propeller tones.
Segmentation: For recordings longer than 30 seconds, the signal is segmented into overlapping 3-second windows (50% overlap) to produce statistically independent analysis frames while maintaining context.
Before/after waveform comparison plots are saved to outputs/waveforms/ for quality control inspection.

Stage II — Spectral Analysis and Feature Extraction (Phases 3-4)
Phase 3: Spectral Analysis
Four complementary spectral representations are computed for every preprocessed signal:

FFT Analysis
Window: Hanning (N_FFT = 4096 default)
Resolution: Δf = F_s / N_FFT  (e.g., 11.72 Hz at F_s = 48000 Hz)
Output: Magnitude spectrum |X(k)|, dominant peaks with frequencies and amplitudes
Identifies discrete line spectrum components from rotating machinery harmonics

STFT / Spectrogram
librosa.stft() with N_FFT=2048, hop_length=512
Time-frequency resolution: Δt = hop_length/F_s, Δf = F_s/N_FFT
Mel Spectrogram: 128 mel bands, frequency range 10 Hz – 8 kHz
Captures non-stationarity: engine load changes, speed transients, sea-state variations

Welch PSD
scipy.signal.welch() with nperseg=4096, noverlap=2048
Output: Power Spectral Density S_xx(f) in dB/Hz
Averaged over all segments: variance reduced by factor K (number of segments)
Primary input to NMF decomposition and machinery signature matching

Stage III — Acoustic Signature Discovery (Phases 5-6)
Phase 5: Class-Level Signature Templates
For each vessel class, all individual PSD profiles are averaged element-wise to produce a class-representative spectral template. Overlaid four-class comparison plots reveal the class-discriminating frequency bands. These per-class templates serve as prior information for NMF initialisation (seeding the basis matrix W with known class spectral shapes accelerates convergence and improves interpretability).

Phase 6: Machinery Signature and BPF Detection
A harmonic sweep algorithm scores candidate fundamental frequencies against observed PSD peaks:
For each candidate fundamental f_0 ∈ {10, 12, 15, 18, 20, 25, 30, 40, 50, 60, 80, 100, 120, 150, 180, 200} Hz:
Compute harmonic set H_k = {f_0, 2f_0, 3f_0, 4f_0, 5f_0, 6f_0}.
For each harmonic, check if a PSD peak exists within ±Δf/2 of that frequency.
Score = count of matched harmonics / 6. Highest-scoring candidates are retained as machinery signatures.
Propeller Blade Passing Frequency: BPF = N_blades × RPM / 60. For typical ships, BPF ranges from 3 Hz (slow tugs) to 50 Hz (fast cargo vessels). BPF harmonics produce the characteristic line spectrum. Detection is performed by identifying equally-spaced PSD peaks via autocorrelation of the PSD.

Stage IV — Source Decomposition (Phases 7-8)
Phase 7: NMF-Based Source Estimation (Classical Baseline)
NMF is applied to the PSD matrix to extract R=3 to R=8 latent spectral components (number selected by BIC criterion). Component spectra W[:,r] are inspected and labelled against known machinery spectral profiles:
Low-frequency tonal component (< 100 Hz): engine shaft rotation and propeller
Mid-frequency tonal component (100-500 Hz): auxiliary machinery (generators, pumps, compressors)
Broadband component: cavitation and hydrodynamic noise
The NMF solution provides contribution weights H[r, τ] for each time frame, which are integrated to give scalar weights w_r. Residual signals are passed to Phase 9.

Phase 8: Deep Time-Domain Separator (GenAI Core — Source Separation)
The primary source decomposition engine is a parallel dilated group convolutional network based on the Conv-TasNet paradigm, adapted and extended for ship-radiated noise characteristics. The architecture comprises three modules:

Encoder
1D convolution: K=512 kernels, kernel length L_k=16, stride S=8
Output: Feature matrix A ∈ R^(K × L_f) where L_f = (L_n - L_k)/S + 1
ReLU activation ensures non-negativity
Projects time-domain signal into high-dimensional latent space

Separation Layer (GenAI Core)
Layer normalisation + bottleneck 1x1 convolution (K1=128 kernels)
B=3 convolutional blocks, each containing u=8 convolutional units
Each unit: PARALLEL DILATED GROUP CONVOLUTION
  Path 1: Fixed dilation d1=1 (no expansion) — captures fine temporal detail
  Path 2: Exponential dilation d2=2^i (i=unit index) — captures long-range envelope
Group convolution (U=8 groups) reduces parameter count from 4.43M to 3.67M
Skip connections accumulate features across all units
Output: N separation masks m_i ∈ [0,1]^(K × L_f) for N sources

Decoder
Element-wise multiply: I_i = A ⊙ m_i
1D transposed convolution: recovers time-domain separated signal
Dimension-preserving padding ensures output length = input length
Outputs N separated time-domain signals n_i(t)

Training objective: Scale-Invariant Signal-to-Noise Ratio (SI-SNR):
SI-SNR = 10 log10( ||n_target||² / ||e||² ),    n_target = (<n_hat, n> / ||n||²) · n,    e = n_hat - n_target
Training protocol: Adam optimiser, initial LR = 0.001, 100 epochs, LR halved on 3-epoch validation plateau. Mixed training corpus constructed by additively mixing pairs of different-class DeepShip segments at random SNR in [-5, +5] dB (5000 mixtures, split 70:20:10 train:val:test).

Stage V — Generative AI Modelling (Phases 9-11)
Phase 9: Residual Noise Characterisation
After source decomposition, the residual ε[n] = x[n] - Σw_i*m_i[n] is subjected to the following statistical characterisation pipeline:
Kolmogorov-Smirnov test vs. Normal(0,1) at significance α=0.05.
Shapiro-Wilk test for small samples (n < 5000).
Excess kurtosis κ_4 and skewness γ_1 computation.
Classification: Gaussian (|κ_4| < 0.5), Near-Gaussian (0.5 ≤ |κ_4| < 2), Non-Gaussian (2 ≤ |κ_4| < 5), Heavy-tailed/Impulsive (|κ_4| ≥ 5).
Alpha-stable distribution fitting (scipy.stats.levy_stable) for impulsive residuals.
Histogram plots with overlaid fitted distributions are saved for each file.

Phase 10: Generative AI Noise and Source Modelling
Four complementary generative architectures are trained, targeting different aspects of the acoustic generation problem:

10A. WGAN-GP for Residual Noise Distribution Learning
The WGAN-GP is the primary model for learning the impulsive, non-Gaussian residual noise distribution. Architecture:
Generator G: Input z ~ N(0, I_{z_dim=128}) → Dense(256, LeakyReLU) → Dense(512, LeakyReLU) → Dense(512, LeakyReLU) → Dense(seg_len=256, Tanh) → output residual segment
Discriminator D (Critic): Input real/fake segment (256 samples) → Conv1D(64,4) → Conv1D(128,4) → Conv1D(256,4) → Flatten → Dense(1). NO sigmoid (WGAN outputs raw Wasserstein estimate).
Gradient penalty: λ_GP = 10 on interpolated samples x_hat = α·x_real + (1-α)·x_fake.
Training: Critic updated 5× per generator update. Adam optimiser, LR=0.0001, β1=0.5, β2=0.9.
Convergence: Monitored via Wasserstein distance estimate (critic loss). Early stopping at plateau.

10B. Conditional Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM) for Source Separation
The DDPM is the state-of-the-art generative model for source separation in this framework. It operates on spectrogram patches conditioned on source identity:
Conditioning vector c: one-hot encoded source label (Engine, Propeller, Cavitation, Ambient, Generator, Pump) concatenated with vessel class embedding.
Noise schedule: linear β schedule from β_1=0.0001 to β_T=0.02, T=1000 diffusion steps.
Score network architecture: U-Net backbone with residual blocks, self-attention at 16×16 and 8×8 resolutions, group normalisation, sinusoidal time embedding, cross-attention conditioning on c.
Classifier-Free Guidance: Randomly drop conditioning c with probability p_uncond=0.1 during training. At inference: ε_θ(x_t|c) = (1+w)·ε_θ(x_t|c) - w·ε_θ(x_t|∅), where w=3.0 for strong source fidelity.
Training: AdamW, LR=2e-4, cosine annealing, batch=64 spectrogram patches (64×64 mel bins).
Inference: DDIM sampler (50 steps, η=0) for fast deterministic reconstruction from separation masks.

10C. Variational Autoencoder (VAE) for Latent Source Representation
The VAE learns a compact, continuous latent space for each acoustic source type, enabling controlled interpolation and attribute manipulation:
Encoder q_φ(z|x): Conv1D(32,8)→Conv1D(64,8)→Conv1D(128,4)→Flatten→Dense(512)→[μ_z, log σ²_z] ∈ R^(z_dim=64)
Reparameterisation: z = μ_z + σ_z ⊙ ε, ε ~ N(0,I)
Decoder p_θ(x|z): Dense(512)→Reshape→ConvTranspose1D(128,4)→ConvTranspose1D(64,8)→ConvTranspose1D(1,8)
Loss: L_VAE = -E_q[log p_θ(x|z)] + β · KL(q_φ(z|x) || N(0,I)). β=4 (β-VAE) for disentangled latents.
Latent space analysis: t-SNE visualisation to confirm class-discriminative structure in z-space.

10D. TimeGAN for Temporal Acoustic Sequence Synthesis
TimeGAN learns the joint temporal distribution of acoustic sequences, preserving both marginal distributions and time-series dynamics:
Embedding network: GRU(hidden=128, 3 layers) maps real sequence x_{1:T} to latent trajectory h_{1:T}.
Recovery network: GRU(128, 3 layers) + Dense → reconstructs x_{1:T} from h_{1:T}. L_recon = ||x - R(E(x))||²_2
Generator: Takes random trajectory z_{1:T} ~ N(0,I) → GRU(128,3) → synthetic latent h'_{1:T}.
Discriminator: GRU(128,3) → Dense(1, Sigmoid) → classify real vs. generated latent trajectories.
Supervised loss: L_sup = E[||h_{t+1} - G(h_t, z_t)||²] ensures generator respects conditional temporal dynamics.
Joint loss: L = L_GAN + η_1·L_sup + η_2·L_recon, where η_1=η_2=1.0.

Phase 11: Synthetic Acoustic Corpus Generation
Trained generative models are used to produce a synthetic augmentation corpus:
WGAN-GP generates 10,000 residual noise segments (256 samples each) per vessel class.
Conditional DDPM generates 2,000 spectral patches per source type per vessel class.
TimeGAN generates 5,000 3-second synthetic acoustic sequences across all four vessel classes.
Synthetic signals are mixed with real extracted source components to form new composite training samples: x_synth(t) = Σ w_i*m_i_real(t) + ε_synth(t).
Quality filter: Only synthetic samples with FID (Fréchet Inception Distance) < threshold and KL divergence < 0.1 vs. real distribution are retained.
Final augmented dataset: 2× to 5× expansion of the original DeepShip working subset.

Stage VI — RNL-SBN Mapping, Validation and Classification (Phase 12)
RNL to SBN Mapping Module
The RNL-SBN mapping reconstructs the chain from onboard noise sources through hull transfer function and propagation medium to measured hydrophone level:
RNL(f) = H(f) · [Σ w_i · M_i(f)  +  P(f)  +  C(f)]  +  A(f)  +  E(f)
H(f) is estimated by deconvolution of the estimated machinery component sum from the observed RNL. A minimum-phase assumption allows stable inversion. The propagation term E(f) is modelled using Thorp's absorption formula for the frequency-dependent attenuation coefficient:
α(f) = 0.11f²/(1+f²) + 44f²/(4100+f²) + 3×10⁻⁴f² + 0.003    [dB/km]
This gives the transfer loss TL(r,f) = 20log10(r) + α(f)·r, where r is the estimated hydrophone-to-ship range (obtained from AIS data or estimated from arrival time).

7.  Evaluation Framework and Metrics
7.1  Source Separation Metrics
Following the conventions established in the DeepShip separation literature (He et al., 2024; Vincent et al., 2006):

Metric
Definition and Significance
SNR (Signal-to-Noise Ratio)
10 log10(||n||² / ||n_hat - n||²). Global measure of separation fidelity.
SI-SNR (Scale-Invariant SNR)
SNR computed after projection onto ground truth signal axis. Scale-invariant: robust to amplitude mismatches.
SNRi (SNR Improvement)
SNR(separated) - SNR(mixed input). Measures net improvement attributable to the separation system.
SI-SNRi (SI-SNR Improvement)
SI-SNR(separated) - SI-SNR(mixed). Primary headline metric in recent literature.
SegSNR (Segmental SNR)
Frame-level SNR averaged over all frames. Captures local separation quality and transient fidelity.
PESQ / POLQA (if applicable)
Perceptual quality scores adapted for acoustic signals where a human-perceptual analogue is relevant.

7.2  Generative Model Metrics
Metric
Definition and Target
MSE (Mean Squared Error)
E[||x_real - x_gen||²]. Should converge below baseline MSE of alpha-stable model.
KL Divergence
KL(p_real || p_gen). Asymmetric: penalises generator for missing real distribution modes. Target: < 0.05 nats.
JS Divergence
Symmetric variant of KL. JS(p_real, p_gen) ∈ [0, log2]. Target: < 0.05 bits.
FID (Frechet Inception Distance)
Distance between real and generated spectrogram features in Inception network embedding space. Lower = better quality and diversity.
Wasserstein Distance (W1)
Earth mover's distance between real and generated distributions. Monotonically related to WGAN-GP critic loss.
BER (Bit Error Rate) — if applicable
Communication system performance under generated noise vs. real noise. Should be within 0.5 dB of real noise curve.

7.3  Classification Metrics
Metric
Definition
Top-1 Accuracy
Fraction of test samples correctly classified into one of four vessel classes.
Macro F1-Score
Unweighted mean of per-class F1 = 2PR/(P+R). Robust to class imbalance.
Confusion Matrix
Per-class assignment distribution. Reveals systematic confusions (e.g., Cargo vs. Tanker).
ROC-AUC (per class, OvR)
Area under ROC curve in one-vs-rest setting. Measures probabilistic discrimination quality.
Delta Accuracy (ΔAcc)
Accuracy(augmented) - Accuracy(baseline). Quantifies GenAI augmentation contribution.

9.  Project Deliverables
Upon completion, the project will produce five primary operational deliverables:

SBN → RNL Mapping Module: A calibrated transfer function model mapping individual onboard noise sources to measured hydrophone RNL. Includes frequency-dependent hull transfer function H(f) estimates per vessel class and propagation loss model for the operating environment.

Machinery Contribution Weight Estimator: A trained model (Generative Mixture Model + Transformer encoder) that accepts a new hydrophone recording and outputs per-source contribution weights w_i with associated uncertainty bounds. Includes harmonic BPF detection for propeller RPM estimation.

Residual Noise Model: Trained WGAN-GP and DDPM models capturing the statistical distribution of non-Gaussian residual noise for each vessel class. Deployable for BER testing of naval acoustic communication systems and for generating realistic environmental noise in simulation.

Synthetic Acoustic Generator: A Python API wrapping the trained TimeGAN and conditional DDPM models, capable of generating configurable-duration synthetic hydrophone recordings for any vessel class, specified RPM range, and sea state condition. Critical for training data augmentation without operational risk.

Improved Ship Classification System: An ensemble classifier (CNN + CRNN + Transformer with confidence-weighted voting) trained on the augmented corpus, with expected accuracy improvements of 5-15% over DeepShip baselines. Packaged as a Docker container with REST API endpoint for real-time operational deployment.

Per-file waveform, FFT, PSD, spectrogram plots — visual QA corpus
Training loss curves and convergence diagnostics for all GenAI models
Ablation study results table and confusion matrices
Research paper draft ready for journal submission (target: IEEE Transactions on Geoscience and Remote Sensing / Journal of Marine Science and Engineering)

High / Medium

Hull transfer function estimation error
Medium / Medium
Estimate H(f) from multiple recordings of the same vessel class; apply regularisation (Tikhonov) in deconvolution; report confidence bounds on H(f).

Low / Medium

High / High
Data augmentation (primary); dropout 0.5, weight decay 1e-4; k-fold cross-validation (k=5); early stopping on validation loss.
Computational budget exceeded by diffusion model
Medium / High
